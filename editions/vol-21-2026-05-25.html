The Bronze Age Collapse

In 1177 BC, the Mediterranean world was a masterpiece of rigid permanence. The great empiresÔÇöEgypt, the Hittites, the MycenaeansÔÇörelied on massive centralized bureaucracies and heavy chariot warfare. They were the ultimate industrial-era pipelines of antiquity. Then came the Sea Peoples. They were not a unified superpower but a fluid, iterative swarm of decentralized raiders. They abandoned heavy chariots for light infantry and rapid coastal strikes, bypassing the fortified walls of the old world. Within decades, centuries of entrenched geopolitical architecture collapsed into dust. The ancient empires failed because they mistook their massive weight for invulnerability. They could not adapt to a threat that refused to play by their static rules. The lesson remains etched in the ruins of antiquity: when the environment demands speed and iteration, the heaviest armor becomes a tomb.
